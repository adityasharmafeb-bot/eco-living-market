Problem

Most entry-level marketing resumes describe tools without proving hands-on skill — anyone can list "GA4" as a skill. I wanted a portfolio piece that instead demonstrates the full loop: build a real interface, instrument it correctly, and show the data it actually produces. Eco Living Market is that piece — a self-directed exercise in setting up performance marketing measurement the way an in-house analytics or media team would for a real client.

The funnel shows 100% abandonment between page view and purchase across both the 1-day and 28-day windows. But this isn't a behavioral drop-off — it's a tracking gap. The site has working Add to Cart, Cart, and Checkout pages, but GA4 is only capturing default auto-collected events, not the add_to_cart / begin_checkout ecommerce events needed to see where in the mid-funnel users actually are. Separately, the traffic channel report shows 5 Paid Search sessions I can't yet explain, since no live campaign is running — flagged for audit rather than cited as a real acquisition channel.

Recommendation

What I'd act on next if this were a live store, based on the data above:

  1. Instrument the missing mid-funnel events first — add add_to_cart and begin_checkout as real GTM-tracked ecommerce events before drawing any behavioral conclusions. This is a tracking fix, not a UX fix.
  2. Audit the Paid Search entry in Traffic Acquisition — with no live campaign running, it likely reflects GA4's default channel grouping misclassifying referral or direct traffic. Confirm before citing it as a real source.
  3. Re-run the funnel once mid-funnel events are live, then decide whether the real leak point calls for a CTA/UX fix or an acquisition fix — the data doesn't support either conclusion yet.

How this proves the skill

I built every page shown here — including the Cart and Checkout pages — and connected the GA4 property myself. When the funnel came back at 100% abandonment, I didn't take that as a UX finding. I checked what was actually being tracked, found the mid-funnel ecommerce events weren't instrumented yet, and traced the number back to a measurement gap instead of guessing at a redesign. I also caught a channel discrepancy in my own data — 5 Paid Search sessions with no live campaign running — and flagged it as unverified rather than citing it as fact. That's the loop a performance marketing analyst runs on real client data: don't trust a number until you know why it's there.
